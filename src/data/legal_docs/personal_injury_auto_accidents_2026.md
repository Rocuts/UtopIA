# Personal Injury & Auto Accident Law in the United States (2026)

This document covers U.S. personal injury law as it applies to motor vehicle accidents, including car crashes, truck accidents, motorcycle collisions, pedestrian injuries, and rideshare incidents. It addresses both general personal injury claims AND work-related vehicle accidents covered under Workers' Compensation.

## 1. Fault vs. No-Fault Insurance Systems

The United States operates under two primary systems for car accident claims:

### Fault (Tort) States
In fault states, the party responsible for causing the accident must pay for damages. The injured party files a claim against the at-fault driver's insurance. Most U.S. states operate under this system, including **Texas, California, Illinois, Georgia, Ohio, and Arizona**.

### No-Fault States (PIP Required)
In no-fault states, each driver's own insurance covers their injuries regardless of who caused the crash. This coverage is called **Personal Injury Protection (PIP)**. The no-fault states as of 2026 are: **Florida, Hawaii, Kansas, Kentucky, Massachusetts, Michigan, Minnesota, New Jersey, New York, North Dakota, Pennsylvania, and Utah**.

- In **Kentucky, New Jersey, and Pennsylvania**, drivers can opt out of no-fault and retain the right to sue.
- **Florida 2026 Update:** Florida is transitioning away from its no-fault PIP system. The full repeal of mandatory PIP takes effect **July 1, 2026**, moving Florida to a bodily injury liability-based system similar to Georgia and Texas.

### When You Can Sue in a No-Fault State
Drivers in no-fault states can file lawsuits only if injuries meet certain severity thresholds:
- **Verbal threshold:** Injuries must be described as serious (e.g., permanent disfigurement, loss of bodily function).
- **Monetary threshold:** Medical bills must exceed a specific dollar amount (e.g., Massachusetts requires damages exceeding $2,000).

## 2. Comparative Negligence Rules

When both parties share blame, state negligence laws determine compensation:

### Pure Comparative Negligence
States like **California, New York, and Florida (pre-2024)** allow you to recover damages even if you are 99% at fault, though your award is reduced by your percentage of fault.

### Modified Comparative Negligence (50% or 51% Bar)
States like **Illinois, Texas, and Florida (post-2024)** bar recovery if you are 50% or 51% or more at fault. Florida adopted modified comparative negligence under HB 837 (signed March 2023), barring injury victims found more than 50% at fault from recovering damages.

### Contributory Negligence
In a few states (**Alabama, Maryland, North Carolina, Virginia, and Washington D.C.**), if you are found even 1% at fault, you recover nothing.

## 3. What to Do After a Car Accident (Step-by-Step)

1. **Ensure safety:** Move to a safe location if possible. Turn on hazard lights.
2. **Call 911:** Report the accident and request police and medical assistance if needed.
3. **Document everything:** Take photos and videos of all vehicles, damage, road conditions, traffic signs, skid marks, and any visible injuries from multiple angles.
4. **Exchange information:** Get the other driver's name, address, phone number, insurance company, policy number, and license plate.
5. **Get witnesses:** Collect names and contact information of any witnesses.
6. **Get the police report:** Ask the responding officer for the report number and how to obtain a copy.
7. **Seek medical attention:** Visit a doctor within 24–72 hours even if you feel fine. Some injuries (whiplash, concussions, internal bleeding) have delayed symptoms.
8. **Report to your insurance:** Notify your insurer within 24–72 hours to avoid claim denial.
9. **File a DMV report if required:** Many states require reporting to the DMV within 10–30 days if injuries occurred or damage exceeds a threshold (e.g., California requires SR-1 form within 10 days if anyone was injured or damage exceeds $1,000).
10. **Consult a personal injury attorney:** Especially if there are serious injuries, disputed fault, or if the other driver is uninsured.

## 4. Types of Damages (Compensation)

### Economic Damages (Special Damages)
Measurable financial losses:
- **Medical expenses:** Emergency room, hospital stays, surgeries, prescriptions, physical therapy, rehabilitation, future medical care.
- **Lost wages:** Income missed during recovery.
- **Loss of earning capacity:** If injuries permanently reduce your ability to work.
- **Property damage:** Vehicle repair or replacement costs.
- **Out-of-pocket costs:** Transportation to medical appointments, home modifications, assistive devices.

### Non-Economic Damages (General Damages)
Intangible harm:
- **Pain and suffering:** Physical pain and discomfort from injuries.
- **Emotional distress:** Anxiety, depression, PTSD, insomnia resulting from the accident.
- **Loss of enjoyment of life:** Inability to participate in hobbies, activities, or daily life.
- **Loss of consortium:** Impact on relationships with spouse or family.

### Punitive Damages
Awarded to punish the defendant for extreme recklessness (e.g., DUI accidents, road rage). Requires proof of malicious intent or gross negligence. Not available in all states and often capped.

## 5. Statute of Limitations by State (Key Examples)

The statute of limitations sets the deadline for filing a personal injury lawsuit. Missing this deadline forfeits your right to compensation.

| State | Personal Injury | Property Damage |
|-------|----------------|-----------------|

| Texas | 2 years | 2 years |
| New York | 3 years | 3 years |
| Florida | 2 years (post-HB 837) | 4 years |
| Illinois | 2 years | 5 years |
| New Jersey | 2 years | 6 years |
| Pennsylvania | 2 years | 2 years |
| Ohio | 2 years | 2 years |

**Tolling exceptions:** The clock may pause if the injured party is a minor (until they reach age of majority), is mentally incapacitated, or if the defendant leaves the state.

## 6. Work-Related Vehicle Accidents (Workers' Compensation Intersection)

**CRITICAL:** If a car accident happens while working, the injured worker has BOTH Workers' Compensation rights AND potential personal injury claims.

### When Workers' Compensation Covers a Car Accident
- The employee was **driving as part of their job duties** (delivery drivers, truck drivers, salespeople, ride-share drivers on active trips, couriers, field technicians).
- The employee was **running a work errand** for the employer.
- The employee was **traveling between job sites** (not home-to-work commute).

### The "Coming and Going" Rule
Generally, accidents during the **regular commute** (home to work and back) are NOT covered by Workers' Compensation. However, exceptions exist:
- If the employer provides transportation.
- If the employee was running a work errand during the commute.
- If the employee has no fixed workplace (traveling workers).
- If the commute involves a special hazard related to employment.

### Dual Claims (Workers' Comp + Personal Injury)
If a **third party** (not the employer or coworker) caused the accident, the injured worker may file:
1. A **Workers' Compensation claim** for medical bills and lost wages (no-fault, so negligence doesn't matter).
2. A **personal injury lawsuit** against the at-fault third party for full damages (including pain and suffering, which Workers' Comp does not cover).

### Employer Liability
Employers may be directly liable if:
- They **failed to maintain company vehicles**.
- They **pressured employees to drive long hours** without rest.
- They did not provide **proper safety training**.
- They required employees to use **unsafe routes or vehicles**.

## 7. Rideshare Accidents (Uber / Lyft) — 2026

Insurance coverage for rideshare accidents depends on the driver's app status:

### Period 1: App Off
Only the driver's personal auto insurance applies. Uber/Lyft provide no coverage.

### Period 2: App On, Waiting for Ride Request
Uber/Lyft provide contingent liability coverage (only if personal insurance denies the claim):
- **$50,000** per person for bodily injury.
- **$100,000** per accident for bodily injury.
- **$30,000** for property damage.

### Period 3: Ride Accepted or Passenger in Vehicle
Uber/Lyft's **$1 million** third-party liability policy is active. Contingent comprehensive and collision coverage also applies if the driver carries those on their personal policy.

### 2026 Update (California)
California reduced TNC (Transportation Network Company) UM/UIM requirements from $1 million per trip to **$60,000 per person / $300,000 per incident** effective January 1, 2026.

## 8. Uninsured and Underinsured Motorist Claims

If the at-fault driver has no insurance or insufficient coverage:
- **Uninsured Motorist (UM) coverage** on your own policy covers your injuries and damages.
- **Underinsured Motorist (UIM) coverage** fills the gap between the at-fault driver's policy limits and your actual damages.
- Many states require UM/UIM coverage. Check your policy.

## 9. Rights of Undocumented Immigrants in Auto Accidents

**Regardless of immigration status**, all individuals in the United States have the legal right to:
- **File a personal injury claim** if injured by another party's negligence.
- **Seek full compensation** for medical expenses, lost wages, pain and suffering, and property damage.
- **File a police report** without fear of immigration consequences.
- **Access emergency medical treatment** under EMTALA (Emergency Medical Treatment and Labor Act).

### Key Protections
- Filing a personal injury claim **does not trigger deportation proceedings**.
- **ICE and USCIS are barred** from targeting individuals for pursuing legal action.
- Immigration status is generally **inadmissible as evidence** in personal injury cases in many states.
- Courts have consistently held that immigration status does not negate the right to legal redress.

### If the Accident Was Work-Related
Undocumented workers involved in on-the-job vehicle accidents are fully protected by Workers' Compensation in most states, and by OSHA regulations. Employers cannot retaliate or threaten to report immigration status.

## 10. Common Types of Vehicle Accidents Covered

- **Car-on-car collisions** (rear-end, T-bone, head-on, sideswipe)
- **Truck accidents** (18-wheelers, delivery trucks — may involve federal FMCSA regulations)
- **Motorcycle accidents**
- **Pedestrian hit by vehicle**
- **Bicycle vs. vehicle accidents**
- **Rideshare accidents** (Uber, Lyft — see Section 7)
- **Hit-and-run accidents**
- **DUI/DWI accidents** (potential punitive damages)
- **Road defect / government liability** (potholes, missing signs, construction zones)
- **Defective vehicle parts** (product liability claims against manufacturers)
