# US Immigration and Employment Law Summary (2026)

This document outlines the intersection of United States immigration law and employment rights as of 2026. It is designed to provide guidance to immigrant workers, regardless of their documentation status, and to summarize the laws governing employment-based visas.

## 1. Core Protections for Immigrant Workers (Regardless of Status)

In the United States, basic labor standards apply to **all workers**, whether they are documented, undocumented, or hold a temporary visa. 

### Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) Protections
- **Minimum Wage and Overtime:** Undocumented workers are fully protected by the FLSA. They are legally entitled to the federal (or state, if higher) minimum wage and overtime pay (time-and-a-half for hours worked over 40 in a workweek).
- **Retaliation:** It is illegal for an employer to retaliate against a worker for complaining about unpaid wages. A common illegal retaliation tactic is threatening to call Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE).

### Title VII and EEOC Protections
- **National Origin Discrimination:** Under Title VII of the Civil Rights Act, it is illegal to discriminate against an employee based on their national origin, birthplace, ancestry, culture, or linguistic characteristics (like an accent).
- **Language Policies:** Employers cannot enforce "English-only" rules unless they are an absolute business necessity for safe and efficient operation.
- **Project Firewall (2026):** The EEOC and DOL have partnered aggressively to root out disparate treatment of immigrant workers, especially in construction, hospitality, and agriculture.

### Workplace Occupational Safety and Health (OSHA)
- Immigrant workers have the exact same rights to a safe workplace under OSHA as U.S. citizens. Employers must provide necessary safety gear and training in a language the worker understands.
- Injured undocumented workers are generally eligible for Workers' Compensation limits, though rules vary by state regarding vocational rehabilitation.

## 2. Visas for Victims of Workplace Crimes or Trafficking

Workers who face severe abuse, trafficking, or crimes at the workplace may be eligible for specific visa protections that grant legal status and work authorization.

### U Visas (Victims of Crime)
- Available to immigrants who have suffered substantial mental or physical abuse as a result of certain crimes (including extortion, blackmail, or severe workplace abuse) and are helpful to law enforcement or government officials in the investigation.
- Protections from deportation are provided during the pending application.

### T Visas (Victims of Human Trafficking)
- Designed for victims of severe forms of trafficking in persons (including labor trafficking or forced labor).
- In 2026, enforcement against forced labor of H-2A/H-2B workers has been highly prioritized by the DOJ, making T Visas heavily relevant for exploited agricultural and manual laborers.

### Deferred Action for Labor Enforcement
- Workers who are victims of or witnesses to labor rights violations (e.g., wage theft, unsafe conditions) and who report these to agencies like the DOL or NLRB can apply for temporary protection from deportation and work authorization to aid in the investigation.

## 3. Temporary Employment Visas (2026 Context)

### H-1B (Specialty Occupations)
- **2026 Updates:** The H-1B lottery system is now weighted based on wage levels, prioritizing higher-paid roles.
- **Online Presence Review:** The U.S. State Department requires disclosure of social media identifiers for H-1B workers and dependents.

### H-2A (Temporary Agricultural Workers)
- For seasonal/temporary agricultural labor.
- Employers must provide housing, transportation, and meals (or cooking facilities).
- H-2A workers frequently face labor violations. If an employer confiscates passports or forces labor beyond the contract, the worker may qualify for a T Visa.

### H-2B (Temporary Non-Agricultural Workers)
- For seasonal/temporary non-agricultural work (e.g., landscaping, hospitality, seafood processing).
- Workers are entitled to the prevailing wage and cannot be charged recruitment fees by the employer or their agents.

## 4. Employer Compliance and I-9 Verification

### Form I-9 and E-Verify
- Employers are required to verify the identity and employment authorization of all individuals hired in the U.S. using Form I-9.
- ICE conducts unannounced I-9 inspections. However, during union organizing drives or labor disputes, ICE generally halts enforcement at the worksite to prevent employers from weaponizing immigration enforcement against protesting workers.

### Employment Authorization Documents (EAD)
- **2026 Policy Constraints:** USCIS reduced the validity period for most EAD categories to 18 months, requiring workers to renew earlier to prevent lapses in work authorization.

## 5. State-Level Variations (e.g., California 2026)
- **Workplace Know Your Rights Act (California):** Requires employers to provide annual employee rights notices regarding immigration and allows employees to designate emergency contacts in case of an ICE raid or detention at work. Stricter than federal baselines.
